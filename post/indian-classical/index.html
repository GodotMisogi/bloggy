Observation - Indian Classical Music

I’ve been a guitarist for the past 9 or 10 years, solely focusing on Western music and its theory; I am by no means an expert, but I’m curious about music theory and like to learn of its different applications/interpretations in various styles.

My first real introduction to Indian classical music was when I attended a Remember Shakti concert in 2012. I know that it’s a fusion band, but I learnt the basic rhythmic elements during the concert thanks to my school’s guitar teacher, whom I went to the concert with at the time. For someone who’s mostly concentrated on Western music such as rock, metal, classical and jazz, the structure of the music seemed somewhat alien; a lot of it seemed to be improvised, but one could decipher an underlying theme to it. This is similar to jazz in its structure, but melodically very different.

While I won’t cover the basics of Indian or Western classical music theory here, I will talk about the differences I noticed between the two, providing the relevant introductory terms as the passage goes. There are much better sources to refer to for the basics, such as: Indian, Western.

The most obvious difference is the taxonomy: “Do Re Mi Fa So La Ti Do” becomes “Sa Re Ga Ma Pa Dha Ni Sa” with the same tonal differences or ‘intervals’. A huge advantage of this shift in terminology is that Indian classical singers can make beautiful, fluent melodies simply by singing those syllables; the fluidity is not prevalent in the Western syllables if various combinations of them are sung.
“Modes” are called “Raags” and they do not share the same database in translation, for example: There is no raag corresponding to the Locrian mode of the major scale.
